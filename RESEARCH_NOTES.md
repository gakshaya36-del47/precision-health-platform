# Research Notes

## Research questions
- Can a transparent, weighted (non-black-box) model meaningfully surface
  cross-domain modifiable factors (sleep, diet, activity, stress) without
  overstating certainty?
- How much does adding a small, well-evidenced genetic-susceptibility layer
  change the explanatory value of the model versus lifestyle factors alone?

## Hypotheses
- H1: A subset of modifiable lifestyle factors (sleep quality, physical
  activity, dietary pattern) will rank as high-priority shared factors across
  all four domains more often than any single genetic variant considered here.
- H2: Presenting evidence-graded, gene-diet interaction information (rather
  than a single risk number) will be more consistent with current scientific
  consensus than a single composite score.

## Assumptions
- We assume users provide honest self-report data; no attempt is made to
  verify anthropometric or lifestyle inputs.
- We assume the common variants discussed in Barrea et al. 2020 (FTO, MC4R,
  PPARG, APOE/APOA, ADRB, PLIN, FADS1, CLOCK, MTHFR) are reasonable starting
  points for the genetic layer given their evidence base in obesity/CVD
  nutrigenetics literature; this is not a genome-wide or polygenic-score
  approach.

## Limitations
- Single-paper literature base so far; needs expansion before any evidence
  entry is treated as more than "emerging."
- No real-world or synthetic dataset yet for calibration/validation.

## Unresolved scientific questions
- What screening instrument (or original item set) should the depression
  module use, given copyright constraints on standardized instruments like
  PHQ-9?
- How should the shared-factor engine handle a factor that's protective in one
  domain but has unclear/mixed evidence in another?
